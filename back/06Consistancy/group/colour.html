Colour Theory

The Colour Wheel

A colour circle is made up of 3 colours; Red, yellow and blue. In 1666, Sir Isaac Newton created the first diagram of primary colours. Artists and scientists have designed and studied variations of Newton’s concept. Over the years, there have been disputes over different ways to express the colour wheel but in reality, any logical wheel that shows an arranged sequence can be valid.

In colour theory, the traditional primary colours cannot be formed or mixed by any combination of other colours. All other colours are derived from these three hues. Secondary colours are green, orange and purple. They are formed by mixing the primary colours together. Tertiary colours are made by mixings the primary and secondary colours together.

Color Harmony

Colour harmony is described as a colour arrangement that is pleasing to the eye. It can create a feeling of balance and order to the viewer. Something that is not harmonious can be seen as messy or hectic.

On one side a wrong choice of colour can be seen as so overdone or chaotic it can be hard to look at. On the other side the colours can be son bland that the user is not taking in any of the information presented. The visual requirement is to create of structure that makes sense so that the user can stay engaged in the content, but not tired out from the bombardment of visuals.

Colour in Websites

When talking about the design of a new website, a common question is – “What colours do I use?”. Picking the right colour pallet for your site is vital when it comes to product branding and effectively conveying your idea or message to users. How the colours are viewed can be different in other cultures but colours can mean more than people think at first. Many things could (and should) effect you selection process such as:

  • Target audience
  • Product/service provided
  • Website objective

For example, white is a popular, clean colour but it symbolises death in China. Young people are drawn towards saturated colours when compared with adults who find the brash. The following list is of the potential meaning of colours:

  • Red: Passion, romance, aggression
  • Orange: Excitement, warmth, pessimism
  • Yellow: Brightness, illumination, cowardice
  • Green: Nature, growth, envy
  • Blue: Security, loyalty, sadness
  • Purple: Creativity, royalty, idealistic
  • Black: Power, sophistication, evil
  • White: Pure, clear, empty

Saturation

Saturation is an important factor when talking about website colour. If your site uses product pictures or headlines with important messages, you should pick a desaturated colour as a background in order to make it stand out more. A saturated background will take up the page making the content lost. The pictures will lose their effectiveness, your words will be hard to read and the site hard to navigate. The colours of the product pictures and main headlines should always be more saturated than their respective backgrounds as the will likely draw the user’s eye first.

Too Much Colour

Colour harmony is the most important item to account for when picking a colour for your website. Picking a small number of colours, four or five, plus black and white, should be more than plenty. Too many colours disorientate, distract and annoy the user from why they came to you website in the first place
